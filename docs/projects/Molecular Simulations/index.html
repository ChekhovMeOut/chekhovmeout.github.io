Summary

Background

Overprescription and rapid adaptation have created multidrug-resistant (MDR) bacteria and the possibility of a global health crisis. Credible estimates predict MDR infections could cause 10 million annual deaths by 2050. Although both gram positive and negative bacteria can acquire drug resistances, the latter are particularly worrisome because their double-layered membrane affords more opportunities to reject medication. It is of humanitarian interest to research antibiotic therapies that target gram negative bacteria.

Antimicrobial peptides (AMP) are one candidate. These molecules are endogenously produced in several kingdoms of life to give competitive advantages over other species. AMPs are typically 10 â€“ 50 amino acids long but vary widely in their mechanism of action. Recently, a class of AMPs called bacteriocins have gained academic interest because they effectively kill some members of the phylum Bacteroides. This makes them a promising model for developing a new generation of antibiotics. Bacteriocins function mechanistically by inhibiting the beta-barrel assembly machine (Bam) complex which, somewhat ironically, is used to make outer membrane channels. Bam is critical for cell survival and conserved across all gram negative bacteria. Although the mechanistic understanding of the active site is still incomplete, the A and D subunits are known to be essential.

Objective

This project seeks to explore the docking interactions between bacteriocins and the BamA subunit of various members of phylum Bacteriodes. Both vulnerable (B. fragilis) and resistant (B. vulgatus) wild type strains have been identified, but the structural motifs which underpin these differences are not fully elucidated. Because no pertinent strains have been solved experimentally, unique homology models will need to be constructed and validated. Thiago Monteiro Araujo dos Santos, PhD conceived the original idea and volunteered to be the technical mentor.

Progress

Several homology models have been built using the YASARA twinset. Sequence identity with template alignments sits solidly in the twilight zone (~25%), which complicates model validation. Post-construction refinement methods (YASARA, ModRef) have shown moderate success in improving the model score to about 1 SD (-1.20) from the target resolution. It is possible the trailing, unpacked structure of the POTRA domain is largely responsible for the reduced scoring confidence of the unrefined models. Unfortunately, Swiss-Model's analysis tool Qmean was unable to validate this hypothesis because the target protein is a beta barrel.

Next Steps

I will attempt a more accurate model refinement by using molecular dynamics simulation of membrane lipids, instead of open solution conditions for globular proteins. If the homology models are deemed unsatisfactory or in need of further validation, machine learning methods such as AlphaFold could serve as a viable replacement. Once the final model has been built and validated, I will begin conducting the docking analysis. I plan to use Piper on Schrodinger platform, which is available through the Northeastern University research cluster.
